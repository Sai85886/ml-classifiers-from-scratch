\subsection*{Cross-dataset graph interpretation (explicit requirement coverage)}
Across the four required datasets we include at least 8 graphs; in fact, we include 12 primary model-selection/learning-curve graphs (3 per dataset) plus additional decision-tree train/test histograms for Credit. The key patterns from these graphs are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Convergence / poor local optima (NN):} In some NN learning-curve plots (especially Parkinson's), test cost $J$ decreases slowly and remains above tree/k-NN baselines, suggesting that with small sample size and the chosen training budget, optimization can settle in a suboptimal region or underfit before reaching a better basin.
  \item \textbf{Why numeric-only datasets favor some methods:} On Digits and Rice (purely numeric attributes), distance-based structure is more reliable after z-score normalization, so k-NN and NN are strong; k-NN performs particularly well on Digits because local neighborhoods in pixel space are informative.
  \item \textbf{Mixed/sparse one-hot effects:} On Credit, one-hot expansion creates high-dimensional sparse inputs; k-NN curves are weaker because distance concentration and irrelevant dimensions reduce neighborhood quality, while tree-based methods remain robust by selecting informative splits.
  \item \textbf{Forest-size saturation:} RF accuracy/F1 curves improve rapidly at low-to-moderate \texttt{ntree} and then plateau, consistent with diminishing variance-reduction returns as more trees are added.
  \item \textbf{Bias--variance trend in k-NN curves:} Smaller $k$ often gives high-variance but low-bias boundaries (strong on Parkinson's at $k=1$), while larger $k$ smooths decision boundaries and can reduce overfitting at the cost of bias.
\end{itemize}
